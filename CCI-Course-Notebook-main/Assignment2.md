**READING**

Amy J. Ko and Anne Beitler's thought-provoking article triggered me to think more deeply about the comlexity of connection between technology and society. Here are some points I agree with, but also some points that can be further explored and debated.

Agreed viewpoints: I agree with what the article emphasizes that computing technology and algorithms are not neutral, but are subject to the values, prejudices and social structures of developers. The subjective choices of developers themselves, historical background and prejudices, lack of diversity and inclusive perspectives are all possible limiting factors, which will affect the choices made by designers when designing software, making computing technology reflect some implicit value orientation or prejudice. As Ruha Benjamin mentioned in "Race After Technology", computing technology is not born to solve inequality, on the contrary, it often reshapes inequality. What's more frightening is that this bias may be amplified by technology.

Disagreement viewpoints:
Although the article puts forward many negative effects that algorithms may have and puts forward the basis, it is undeniable that technology also has irreplaceable advantages and possibilities for development, which are barely not mentioned in the article. Although technology can indeed exacerbate inequality, technology is only a carrier, and it is people who exert subjective initiative. Sherry Turkle discussed the complexity of the relationship between humans and technology in her book Alone Together. She acknowledged the complexity of the relationship between humans and technology, but also emphasized that technology can bring positive connections and innovations. Therefore, I believe that technology is neutral and depends more on how it is designed and used. As long as there is timely and appropriate intervention, follow-up and supervision, technology can play a good role and contribute to society.
