**READING**

Agree:
I strongly agree that criticism is not the same as accusation or negative evaluation. It is not only about picking out the shortcomings of an artwork, but also about seeking a deeper understanding of the artwork. Criticism points out deficiencies or technical limitations, but also expands more possibilities and gives constructive feedback and insights, because such feedback can prompt artists to improve and challenge their own boundaries, and can also provide more ideas, thereby achieving higher-dimensional progress.
At the same time, the author also mentioned that there is a lack of effective criticism and discussion in fields such as new media art and generative art. Because it involves emerging fields, many traditional art critics lack this knowledge background. In my opinion, if new forms of criticism are broadened, this situation will be alleviated. For example, criticism can integrate interdisciplinary knowledge and perspectives, so that such works can receive more in-depth comments and understanding.

Disagree:
It is difficult to get more artists to participate in criticism and mutual evaluation in practice. First of all, not everyone is willing to evaluate or has the knowledge and professional ability to criticize. A good creator does not mean he is a good critic. Professional matters should still be left to professionals. Secondly, criticism requires neutrality and objectivity. There may be personal relationships and conflicts of interest among artists. Mutual evaluation will make the evaluation less objective, which may be considered "biased". Therefore, I don’t think this is a good idea.

**REFLECTING**

Jared Tarbell's Substrate uses programming to create a visual effect similar to a city block. The thin lines and geometric shapes in the picture are intertwined, and the sense of order and randomness coexist, giving people a unique sense of beauty. From what I understand, he mainly set the random starting and ending points, random extension angles, and handle edge collisions. These provided some ideas and references for my coding this week, aiming to reflect the effect of coexistence of order and randomness. Therefore, I used a unified circle as the basic element, and made a series of random settings in size, color, walking path, etc., while keeping them extending and moving within a fixed range.
