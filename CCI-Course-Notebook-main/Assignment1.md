### Reading

Jer Thorp's thought-provoking article triggered me to think more deeply about the intersection of technology and art. Here are some points I agree with, but also some points that can be further explored and debated.

One key point Thorp makes is facial recognition technology and "soft selling" surveillance. Here Thorp criticized Google's Arts and Culture app, which uses facial recognition technology to match user selfies with artworks. Although the feature has been welcomed by many people, he pointed out that it is actually "soft selling" facial recognition technology, allowing users to inadvertently accept this technology that is essentially surveillance. I partially agree with Thorp's point of view, because under the packaging of entertainment and art, ignoring the privacy and security risks it may bring in real life.This indicates when we appreciate the artistic innovation brought by technology, we must also be cautious about the potential ethical risks.

However, we cannot deny the fact that in today's digital age, it’s not realistic to completely abandon these technological platforms. Artists still need to use these technologies to some extent to convey their works to more audiences. The key to the problem may be to use reasonable means when using the platform to maintain respect for the privacy and rights of the audience, rather than completely rejecting these media.

Meanwhile, this also involves the definition of artist's social responsibility.Donna Haraway (1985) proposed in "A Cyborg Manifesto" that when artists explore new areas, they should reflect on the ethical consequences of the use of technology and symbols.Although this vigilance is necessary, I think it also requires balanced consideration, because technological innovation is often accompanied by unforeseen consequences, and not all consequences can be anticipated in advance,the responsibility of artists may be more to actively respond to these problems in the subsequent stages.
