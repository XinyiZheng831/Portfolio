**READING**

Agree: I agree that programming and artistic creation have certain commonalities. Both are created from scratch and need inspiration to drive them. Programmers design algorithms and functions, and artists design visual and emotional experiences. Both express themselves and reflect their values. At the same time, I agree that both are a process of repeated modification and improvement. Painters need to modify their works to achieve better artistic effects. Programmers need to continuously optimize the code to make it more concise and efficient. Both need to strive for excellence. Although the modification process and method are different, the purpose is to achieve better results.

Disagree: In the essay, the author overemphasized the commonality between the two and ignored the obvious differences between them. For example, there are completely different requirements for the creation of the two. Programming requires high logic and functionality and strict compliance with specifications, while artistic creation does not require too many rules and regulations and encourages free expression. Casey Reas and Ben Fry pointed out in <Processing A Programming Handbook for Visual Designers and Artists> that programming solves technical limitations and functional requirements, while artistic creation focuses more on sensory effects and audience experience. The two are independent and interdependent. Simply comparing and combining the two will weaken each other's characteristics and functions.

At the same time, I do not agree with the author's over-emphasis on the loneliness and individuality of hackers. This only represents the behavior of some groups, not that all programmers lack communication and cooperation. This is unrealistic in today's social context where division of labor and cooperation are required. The same is true for art. Not all great art is the result of an individual. Elliot Aronson emphasized the impact of social relationships on human psychology and creativity in his book <The Social Animal>, reflecting that innovation and creation are usually achieved in collective and social interactions. Therefore, the image of a lonely hacker is somewhat limited and overly idealized.
