## Explain your game elements and how to play your game, include images

Element Quest is a light strategy board-game set on one long path that moves through four zones: Fire, Wood, Wind, and Water. Players use four kinds of Element cards for normal attacks. Every card has a color element and follows a fixed rock-paper-scissors loop: Fire beats Wood, Wood beats Wind, Wind beats Water, Water beats Fire. The deck also holds two kinds of Spell cards:

General Spells have no element and give simple effects like draw, shield, or move.

Element Spells carry an element tag and interact with the map— for example, a Wood Wall blocks the road until someone burns it away with a Fire card. Smart timing of the right element gives bonus damage or clears these obstacles.

Each zone ends with a matching Element Boss. To pass, a team must play a set number of that Boss’s element cards, but using its counter-element counts double and saves cards. Defeating a Boss lets you draw an Equipment card that gives a permanent bonus and an extra power when you are in its home element or pay a matching card. Collect cards, exploit counters, win gear, beat the four Bosses, and race to the finish to claim victory.

## These are the things it made me think about games in general:

Making Element Quest helped me understand what makes a game fun, fair, and easy to play. First, I learned that one card can do many things. In our game, Element cards are used for attacking, for casting spells, and for defeating Bosses. This makes the game easier to learn, because players don’t have to remember too many rules.

Second, I saw how useful a simple element loop can be. The cycle of Fire > Wood > Wind > Water is easy to remember, and it helps players make good choices. It feels satisfying to use the right element at the right time.

Third, the Boss gates at the end of each region show clear progress. When players defeat a Boss and flip the card, it feels like a big moment. It keeps the game exciting from start to finish.

Most importantly, I learned that a game must be balanced. It should never feel like one player is too strong while others have no chance. In our design, even if someone is behind, they can use counter elements or instant spells to make a comeback. This gives everyone hope and keeps the game fun.

## These are the ideas it gave me about games

1. Good games reuse simple rules. In our game, one Element card can do many things. It keeps the game easy to learn but full of choices.	
2. Easy-to-understand systems help players. The element loop is clear and helps players think about their next move.	
3. Players need goals and feedback. Boss fights in each zone give players a clear goal and sense of progress.	
4. Balance makes games fun. A game is better when all players have a chance. If one side is too strong, others may stop trying.

## Reading & Reflection

These three readings reframed how I think about games—not as mere distractions, but as tools to engage, interrogate, and reimagine the world. Sicart’s idea that “play is a way of being” made me question whether my own game designs truly honor the player’s agency. Bogost’s framing of games as media that “do things” rather than just represent ideas gave games a kind of rhetorical power I hadn’t fully appreciated. But it was Marcotte’s piece that lingered with me most: the idea that even the feel and shape of a controller can be an act of queering shook up how I view interfaces. Game design isn’t just mechanics—it’s politics, bodies, and resistance encoded in form.
